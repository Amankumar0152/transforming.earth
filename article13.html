Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS): A Key Technology in Combating Climate Change Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) is a critical technology designed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from industrial sources and power plants by capturing carbon dioxide (CO2) before it enters the atmosphere and storing it securely underground. As the world grapples with the urgent need to mitigate climate change, CCS is increasingly recognized as an essential tool for reducing CO2 emissions from sectors where other mitigation strategies may be challenging to implement.

Understanding Carbon Capture and Storage

1. Capture: CO2 is separated from other gases produced in industrial processes or from power generation. This can be done using several methods: Post-combustion capture: CO2 is captured after the fossil fuel has been burned. The CO2 is separated from the flue gas, which is the mixture of gases emitted by the plant. This method is widely applicable, especially for retrofitting existing power plants.

Pre-combustion capture: In this method, fossil fuels are partially oxidized to produce a mixture of hydrogen and CO2. The CO2 is then captured before combustion, and the hydrogen can be used as a clean fuel. This method is commonly used in industrial processes like gasification.

Oxy-fuel combustion: The fuel is burned in pure oxygen instead of air, producing a flue gas that is mainly CO2 and water vapor. The water vapor is condensed, leaving almost pure CO2 for capture.

2. Transport: Once captured, the CO2 is compressed and transported to a storage site. This transportation typically occurs through pipelines, which are the most economical method for large quantities of CO2. However, CO2 can also be transported by ship, truck, or rail, depending on the location and scale of the CCS project.

3. Storage: The final step involves injecting the captured CO2 into deep geological formations for long-term storage. These formations could include depleted oil and gas fields, deep saline aquifers, or unmineable coal seams. The CO2 is injected into these formations, where it is stored under layers of impermeable rock that prevent it from escaping back into the atmosphere.

The Importance of CCS in Climate Mitigation CCS is considered a vital component of global efforts to reduce CO2 emissions and limit global warming to below 2°C, as outlined in the Paris Agreement. Here are key reasons why CCS is important:

1. Decarbonizing Hard-to-Abate Sectors: Certain industries, such as cement, steel, and chemical manufacturing, emit large quantities of CO2 as a byproduct of chemical reactions that are integral to their processes. These emissions are difficult to reduce using renewable energy alone. CCS offers a solution for capturing these emissions directly at the source, enabling these sectors to reduce their carbon footprint significantly.

2. Supporting the Transition to Clean Energy: While renewable energy sources like wind, solar, and hydropower are crucial for reducing reliance on fossil fuels, they currently cannot meet the entire global energy demand, especially given the intermittency of renewable energy. Fossil fuel power plants equipped with CCS can provide a stable, low-carbon energy supply during the transition to a fully renewable energy system.

3. Negative Emissions: CCS can be combined with bioenergy (referred to as BECCS – Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage) to create a system that removes CO2 from the atmosphere. In BECCS, biomass (organic material) is used as a fuel, and the CO2 produced during its combustion is captured and stored. Since the biomass absorbs CO2 from the atmosphere during its growth, this process results in a net removal of CO2 from the atmosphere, achieving negative emissions.

4. Enhancing Oil Recovery: In some cases, captured CO2 can be used for Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR), where it is injected into oil fields to increase oil production. This not only helps reduce the overall CO2 emissions but also offsets some of the costs associated with CCS by providing an economic return through increased oil recovery.

5. Addressing Climate Targets: The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and other climate experts have highlighted that achieving global climate targets, particularly limiting warming to 1.5°C, may not be feasible without CCS. This is especially true if the world continues to rely on fossil fuels in the near to medium term. CCS is one of the few technologies available that can remove large quantities of CO2 from the atmosphere, making it a critical tool in the fight against climate change.

Challenges and Criticisms of CCS
Despite its potential, CCS faces several challenges and criticisms:

1. Cost: One of the main barriers to widespread adoption of CCS is its high cost. The technology is expensive, both in terms of the initial investment required to build CCS infrastructure and the ongoing operational costs. The economic feasibility of CCS often depends on government subsidies, carbon pricing mechanisms, or the potential for revenue generation through Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR).

2. Energy Intensity: The process of capturing, transporting, and storing CO2 requires a significant amount of energy, which can reduce the overall efficiency of power plants and industrial processes. This additional energy demand can offset some of the emissions reductions achieved by CCS, especially if the energy used comes from fossil fuels.

3. Long-Term Storage Security: There are concerns about the long-term security of CO2 storage, particularly the risk of leakage from geological formations. While extensive research and monitoring have shown that well-selected and managed storage sites can securely contain CO2 for thousands of years, the possibility of leakage, though low, remains a concern.

4. Public Perception and Acceptance: Public acceptance of CCS projects can be a challenge, particularly when it comes to the storage of CO2 in underground formations. Communities near proposed storage sites may have concerns about the safety and environmental impact of CCS, leading to opposition and delays in project development.

5. Delay in Transition to Renewables: Some critics argue that investing in CCS could prolong the use of fossil fuels by providing a justification for continued fossil fuel extraction and use. They contend that resources would be better spent on accelerating the transition to renewable energy sources and energy efficiency measures.

The Future of CCS Despite these challenges, CCS is likely to play a significant role in global climate mitigation strategies, particularly in scenarios where deep emissions reductions are required. Continued investment in research and development is essential to reduce costs, improve efficiency, and enhance the safety and reliability of CCS technologies.

Government policies and incentives will also be crucial in driving the adoption of CCS. Carbon pricing, subsidies, and regulations that mandate emissions reductions can create the economic conditions necessary for CCS projects to be viable. Public-private partnerships and international collaboration can further accelerate the deployment of CCS by sharing knowledge, resources, and best practices.

As the world works towards achieving net-zero emissions by the middle of the century, CCS will be an important tool in the broader portfolio of climate solutions. While it is not a silver bullet, it offers a way to significantly reduce CO2 emissions from some of the most challenging sectors, supporting the global effort to limit the impacts of climate change and protect the planet for future generations.
